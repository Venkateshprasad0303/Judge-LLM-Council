Council of LLMs

A 7-phase adversarial multi-agent pipeline for legal reasoning — where competing AI models argue, cross-examine, and deliberate before a Chief Judge renders a structured, citation-verified ruling.

7-Phase Pipeline 23 LLM Calls Citation Verified Dynamic Tavily Search 5 AI Providers

What is this and why does it exist

Legal AI is a domain where hallucinations are not just wrong — they are dangerous. A model that confidently cites a non-existent statute or misattributes a legal precedent can lead users to make consequential decisions on false grounds. Most legal AI tools ask a single model a question and return whatever it generates. That model has no adversary, no fact-checker, and no one to call out its assumptions.

Council of LLMs is built on a different premise: structured adversarial disagreement produces more reliable output than any single model's confident answer. The system makes three AI agents argue opposing legal positions, forces them to cross-examine each other, sends their work to a specialist judge panel, and then has a Chief Judge synthesize everything into a final ruling — with every factual claim traced back to a verified source.

Core Design Principle The goal is not to replace legal counsel. It is to reason about legal situations with a level of rigor, source-tracing, and epistemic transparency that a single AI model cannot achieve on its own. Every claim in the output carries a provenance marker: settled, disputed, or single-source.

The problem with single-model legal AI

  • No adversarial pressure — a model arguing one side with no opponent will not surface its own weakest points
  • No citation discipline — without enforcement, models invent case names, statute numbers, and holdings
  • No epistemic grading — output doesn't distinguish "everyone agrees" from "this is contested"
  • No self-correction loop — the first draft is the final draft

What Council of LLMs does instead

  • Three different AI models argue plaintiff, defense, and neutral positions from the same research base
  • Each argument goes through a Build → Critique → Refine loop before being used
  • All three positions face cross-examination by opposing advocates
  • A dedicated consensus measurement step quantifies how much the advocates agreed
  • Three specialist sub-judges (Fact, Law, Precedent) analyze different dimensions in parallel
  • The Chief Judge only reads structured outputs from all prior phases — not the raw query
  • Every citation in the final ruling is programmatically verified against the research bundle
  • A Devil's Advocate runs after the ruling to surface what the Chief Judge might have missed

Architecture

The pipeline is sequential at the phase level but parallel within phases. Each phase produces a structured JSON bundle that becomes the input for the next phase. No phase receives the raw user query directly except Phase 1 — downstream phases only see what prior phases have already structured and validated.

The Seven Phases

1
Intelligence Agent
Sonnet 4.6 with adaptive extended thinking · Reads query + conversation history

Before any research or argumentation begins, the Intelligence Agent reads the user's query and converts it into a structured IntelBundle. This is the most important phase for the quality of everything that follows — if case classification is wrong, every downstream phase inherits that error.

Extended thinking is enabled here with an adaptive budget (3,000–12,000 tokens depending on complexity). For a straightforward landlord-tenant dispute, the model gets 3,000 thinking tokens. For a multi-party constitutional case with conflicting precedent, it gets 12,000. This is important: extended thinking allows the model to reason through ambiguous multi-part queries before committing to a classification, rather than pattern-matching on surface features.

The phase also accepts conversation history (last 10 exchanges). If a user asked about a contract dispute and then follows up with "what if the defendant was a minor?", the Intel Agent detects this is a follow-up, carries forward the existing legalIssues[], and sets isFollowUp: true — preventing the pipeline from re-researching facts it already knows.

Output Schema
caseType, legalIssues[], parties, burdenOfProof, keyFacts[], jurisdiction, complexityLevel, isFollowUp, tavilyConfig
Tavily Routing Decision
The Intel Agent decides how many web searches to fire: 0 for stable settled doctrine (First Amendment text, classical negligence), 3 for moderately volatile law, 6 for evolving areas, 9 for regulatory/immigration/tax where statutes change frequently.
Design Decision Adaptive thinking budgets reduce Phase 1 cost by ~60% for simple queries. A question about "what is the statute of limitations for contract breach in California" does not need the same reasoning depth as "is a non-compete agreement enforceable after a company acquisition that included a change of control clause."
2
Research Fan-out
Haiku ×3 parallel + Sonnet synthesis + Tavily web search

Three Haiku models run simultaneously, each tasked with researching from a different perspective: one looking for plaintiff-favorable authorities, one for defense-favorable, and one for neutral procedural standards. This parallel fan-out is why the research phase doesn't take three times as long as a single call.

Each researcher draws from two sources: the RAG knowledge base (51 real US law documents covering 11 legal domains) and optionally Tavily web search (0–9 real-time searches, decided by Phase 1). The RAG provides stable doctrine — statute text, landmark case holdings, foundational legal tests. Tavily provides recency — recent rulings, regulatory updates, jurisdiction-specific developments.

After the three researchers return, Sonnet 4.6 synthesizes all three perspectives plus the Tavily results into a unified ResearchBundle. Crucially, all source IDs are normalized at this step to src-1, src-2, etc. — stable, predictable identifiers that every downstream phase can cite without inventing new IDs.

Why Source ID Normalization Matters Before this normalization step existed, the synthesizer would generate IDs like "testerman-moulton-1923", and the Chief Judge would later reference something like "testerman1923". The programmatic citation checker would correctly flag this mismatch — but no source actually disappeared. By normalizing to src-N immediately, every downstream model gets clear, unambiguous citation targets.

The ResearchBundle is also prompt-cached using Anthropic's cache-control API. This means the ~17 downstream LLM calls that read the ResearchBundle pay only 10% of the normal input token cost for that content. For a bundle of ~4,000 tokens read 17 times, this saves approximately $0.34 per query.

3
Advocate Chains
3 parallel chains · Each: Build (reasoning model) → Critique (Haiku) → Refine (reasoning model)

This is the core of the adversarial design. Three advocates build arguments from the same ResearchBundle — but they are different AI systems with different underlying training, different reasoning approaches, and different tendencies. The Plaintiff advocate (o4-mini) leans toward formal argumentation. The Defense advocate (Gemini 2.5 Pro) excels at analogical reasoning and finding alternative framings. The Neutral advocate (Sonnet 4.6) focuses on procedural correctness and what the law actually says rather than what a party wants it to say.

Each advocate goes through a three-step chain:

  1. Build — the advocate constructs the strongest argument it can, citing only sources present in the ResearchBundle
  2. Critique — a Haiku model reads the argument against the ResearchBundle and flags any factual inaccuracies, unsupported claims, or sources that don't actually say what the advocate claimed
  3. Refine — the original advocate reads its own argument plus the critique and produces a corrected, strengthened version

Haiku was chosen for the critique step deliberately. It is fast, cheap, and the critique task is purely mechanical: "does this factual claim appear in the ResearchBundle?" The critique does not argue the other side — it only fact-checks.

Why Three Different AI Providers If all three advocates were Anthropic models, they would share the same fine-tuning biases, the same RLHF tendencies, and potentially the same systematic errors. Using o4-mini (OpenAI), Gemini 2.5 Pro (Google), and Sonnet 4.6 (Anthropic) as advocates means disagreements between them are more likely to reflect genuine legal uncertainty rather than coincidental agreement from shared training data.
4
Cross-Examination + Consensus Measurement
Each advocate attacks the other two · Sonnet measures how much they agreed

Each advocate is shown the keyPoints from the other two positions and asked to produce targeted rebuttals — short, specific attack points, each under 20 words. The three rebuttals run in parallel using Haiku. The brevity constraint is deliberate: it forces the model to identify the single most damaging attack rather than producing verbose paragraph-length rebuttals that can drift off-topic.

After the rebuttals, Sonnet 4.6 performs a Consensus Measurement step. It reads all three advocate positions plus all three rebuttals and produces:

  • consensusLevel: one of five levels from STRONG_CONSENSUS to FUNDAMENTAL_DISAGREEMENT
  • divergentClaims[]: the specific factual or legal claims where advocates disagreed
  • agreedFacts[]: facts that all three advocates accepted

The consensusLevel serves two purposes: it directly feeds the Chief Judge's escalation decision in Phase 6, and it appears in the UI as a color-coded badge so the user immediately understands the epistemic state of the case — was this a clear-cut situation or was it genuinely contested?

5
Judge Panel
Three parallel specialist sub-judges · Each given a single narrow question to answer

Instead of asking one judge to handle facts, law, and precedent simultaneously, the pipeline separates these concerns into three specialists running in parallel:

  • Fact Judge (Sonnet 4.6 + extended thinking): Determines which facts are undisputed and which are genuinely contested between the parties. Explicitly instructed not to interpret law. Extended thinking is enabled here because distinguishing disputed from undisputed facts requires careful reading of what each advocate actually claimed versus assumed.
  • Law Judge (o4-mini): Identifies the applicable legal standards, statutes, and formal legal tests. o4-mini's native reasoning mode produces precise, structured doctrinal analysis. Explicitly instructed not to determine facts.
  • Precedent Judge (Gemini 2.5 Pro): Finds analogous cases from the ResearchBundle and prior context and assesses which side each precedent favors. Gemini's strength at analogical pattern-matching makes it well-suited for this role.

If the three sub-judges diverge significantly — for example, if the Precedent Judge says prior cases favor the plaintiff but the Law Judge identifies a statutory defense that overrides those precedents — a minorityFinding flag is set. The Chief Judge sees this flag and must address the disagreement explicitly in its ruling.

6
Chief Judge
Hybrid Sonnet/Opus · Reads every prior phase · Streams output token by token

The Chief Judge is the only model in the pipeline that reads everything. Its input is a structured context block containing: the IntelBundle, the ResearchBundle (cached), the three advocate arguments and rebuttals, the consensusLevel and divergentClaims from Phase 4, and the three sub-judge findings from Phase 5.

The model selection is adaptive. For most queries (~80%), Sonnet 4.6 with a moderate thinking budget is used — it is faster, cheaper, and more than capable of synthesizing well-structured prior phases. Opus 4.6 with a 16,000-token thinking budget is only invoked when two conditions are both true: the case was classified as complex by Phase 1, and the consensus level reached at least SIGNIFICANT_DISAGREEMENT in Phase 4. This hybrid design avoids the $1.20 Opus thinking cost for routine cases while preserving the most capable model for the genuinely hard ones.

The ruling is streamed token by token to the UI. Users see the Chief Judge's output appear in real time while the post-ruling checks in Phase 7 are still being prepared.

Citation Enforcement in the Prompt The Chief Judge's system prompt explicitly lists every valid source ID: "You MUST use ONLY these exact IDs in citedAuthorities and findingsOfFact.sources. Valid IDs: src-1, src-2, src-3... Do NOT invent new IDs. If evidence is thin, state low confidence rather than fabricate." This is the first line of citation defense — before any programmatic check.

The output schema is designed to produce epistemic transparency on every claim:

{
  "findingsOfFact": [{ "fact": "...", "status": "settled|disputed", "sources": ["src-N"] }],
  "conclusionsOfLaw": [{ "conclusion": "...", "basis": "...", "advocateAttribution": "plaintiff|defense|neutral|all" }],
  "ruling": "who prevails and on what grounds",
  "rationale": "detailed reasoning",
  "citedAuthorities": [{ "id": "src-N", "title": "...", "proposition": "..." }],
  "epistemicMarkers": [{ "claim": "...", "status": "settled|disputed|single_source" }],
  "minorityReport": "string or null",
  "modelUsed": "anthropic/claude-sonnet-4.6",
  "escalated": false
}
7
Post-Ruling Checks
Two parallel tasks · Neither modifies the ruling except to replace invalid citations

After the Chief Judge has ruled, two verification tasks run in parallel:

7a. Devil's Advocate Critique — Haiku is given the ruling with a contrarian system prompt: "Aggressively challenge this ruling. Find every weakness, assumption, and potential error." It produces four categories of critique: unquestioned assumptions, missed counterarguments, potential framing biases, and overconfident claims. This appears in the UI as a red-bordered advisory block. It does not change the ruling — it is there so the user knows what the system itself identified as its own weaknesses.

7b. Citation Integrity Check — Every citedAuthority in the ruling is checked against the ResearchBundle's source IDs. The check is programmatic: exact ID match first, then title substring fuzzy match as a fallback. If any citation cannot be verified, Haiku is re-prompted once with the list of valid source IDs and asked to replace the unverified citations with correct ones. The UI shows a green "citations verified" or amber "N citations corrected" indicator.

Why the Re-Prompt Works Rather than failing when the Chief Judge invents a citation, the system has a recovery path: the re-prompt gives Haiku an explicit list of valid IDs and asks it to substitute. This is dramatically cheaper than re-running the Chief Judge (Haiku re-prompt: ~$0.001 vs. Chief Judge: ~$0.30–$1.20).

How hallucinations are reduced

Hallucination in LLMs is not binary — it exists on a spectrum from subtle over-confidence to outright fabrication. The Council of LLMs pipeline addresses this spectrum with multiple overlapping mechanisms rather than any single fix.

Design Philosophy No single mechanism is sufficient. Extended thinking helps but doesn't eliminate hallucinations. Citation enforcement helps but doesn't prevent confident-but-wrong legal analysis. The approach is defense-in-depth: every layer that exists reduces the probability that the output contains a claim that is both wrong and unchallenged.

1. Adversarial architecture — wrong claims get attacked

When the Plaintiff advocate claims that "courts have uniformly held X," the Defense advocate is specifically prompted to attack that claim. If it is wrong or overstated, the cross-examination step will surface it. A claim that survives three advocate positions and three rebuttals is more likely to be well-grounded than a claim from a single model with no adversary.

2. Extended thinking on reasoning-critical phases

Phases 1, 3 (Plaintiff/Defense), 5 (Fact Judge), and 6 (Chief Judge) use extended thinking. This gives the model a separate reasoning scratchpad to work through conflicting information before committing to an answer. In practice, this reduces the confident-but-wrong pattern: models with extended thinking are more likely to express uncertainty when they should.

3. Source ID normalization — citations are auditable

After synthesis, all ResearchBundle sources are relabeled src-1, src-2, etc. Every downstream phase that makes a factual claim must attribute it to one of these IDs. The IDs are a closed set known at generation time — any ID that doesn't match is provably invented.

4. Citation enforcement in the Chief Judge prompt

The Chief Judge's prompt explicitly lists every valid source ID and includes the instruction: "If evidence is thin, state low confidence rather than fabricate." This is the simplest and most direct anti-hallucination intervention: tell the model explicitly what sources exist.

5. Programmatic citation integrity check (Phase 7b)

After the ruling is generated, every cited authority is checked against the ResearchBundle programmatically — no LLM involved in the verification. A citation either matches a known source ID or it does not. If it doesn't match, the re-prompt corrects it.

6. Epistemic markers on every claim

The output explicitly labels each claim as settled (all advocates agreed + 2+ sources), disputed (advocates disagreed, from divergentClaims[]), or single_source (only one ResearchBundle source supports it). These are not post-hoc labels — they are derived from the structured pipeline data, not from the Chief Judge's self-assessment.

7. Haiku critique step in every advocate chain

Before an advocate's argument is used, a separate Haiku model reads the argument against the ResearchBundle and flags any claim that cannot be supported. The advocate must address these in the Refine step. This means factual inaccuracies introduced in the Build step rarely survive to Phase 4.

8. Three-provider independence

Using Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google models as advocates means systematic biases in any one provider's training are contested by two others. If Gemini has a tendency to over-weight certain types of precedents, Sonnet and o4-mini are not subject to that same tendency and will produce a different analysis.

9. Devil's Advocate makes weaknesses explicit

Even after a verified ruling, Phase 7a surfaces the ruling's own weak points. The user sees what the system itself thinks might be wrong. This is epistemic honesty — the system does not present its output as definitive legal authority.

10. Structured JSON at every phase boundary

Every phase consumes and produces structured JSON. Free-text reasoning from one model cannot propagate unchecked into the next phase — it must be parsed into a schema. Fields that don't parse correctly trigger fallback logic or surface an explicit error. This prevents "reasoning drift" where one model's confident prose becomes the unquestioned premise for all subsequent models.

Robust JSON parsing — 7-strategy fallback

LLMs frequently produce nearly-valid JSON: markdown code fences around the output, trailing commas, literal newline characters inside string values, and truncated output when token limits are hit. Rather than failing on any of these, the pipeline uses a sequential 7-strategy parser:

#StrategyHandles
1Strip markdown fences, direct parseClean JSON with ``` wrappers
2Fix literal newlines inside strings, retryUnescaped \n in string values
3Remove trailing commas, retry[...,] and {...,}
4Extract embedded ```json fence, retryThinking models that wrap JSON in a code block inside their response
5Extract outermost { } or [ ], retryProse before/after the JSON object
6Truncation repair: close open strings + bracketsToken-limit truncation mid-JSON string
7Throw with the first 120 characters for debugging—

The truncation repair strategy (step 6) was added after encountering errors like parseJSON failed: { "summary": "Regina v. Dudley and Stephens (1884)... — a response that was cut off mid-string when the model hit its token limit. The repair function walks the JSON character-by-character tracking bracket depth and string state, then closes any open structures to produce parseable output.

Cost Engineering

The first design of this pipeline would have cost approximately $1.96 per query with Opus 4.6 and 16k extended thinking on every call. Through a series of targeted optimizations, the average cost was reduced to approximately $0.73 per query — a 63% reduction — without removing any of the anti-hallucination mechanisms.

Optimization 1 — Prompt caching

The ResearchBundle and IntelBundle are wrapped with Anthropic's cache_control: { type: "ephemeral" } header after Phase 2. Every subsequent LLM call that reads these bundles — 17 calls across Phases 3, 4, 5, and 6 — pays only 10% of the normal input token cost for that cached content. For a typical ResearchBundle of ~4,000 tokens at Sonnet's input rate, this saves approximately $0.34 per query.

Optimization 2 — Adaptive thinking budgets

Extended thinking is expensive: each token in the thinking budget costs the same as an output token. Rather than using a fixed budget for all queries, the pipeline ties the budget to the complexityLevel that Phase 1 assigns:

ComplexitySonnet thinking budgetOpus thinking budget
Simple3,000 tokens6,000 tokens
Moderate6,000 tokens10,000 tokens
Complex12,000 tokens16,000 tokens

Most legal queries are simple or moderate. An easy question about negligence elements gets 3,000 thinking tokens at Phase 1 instead of 16,000 — a 5× cost reduction for that call alone.

Optimization 3 — Hybrid Chief Judge

Opus 4.6 with a 16k thinking budget costs approximately $1.20 per call. This is the single largest cost item in the pipeline. Rather than using it for every query, Opus is only invoked when both conditions are true:

  • Phase 1 classified the case as complex
  • Phase 4 measured consensus at SIGNIFICANT_DISAGREEMENT or FUNDAMENTAL_DISAGREEMENT

For approximately 80% of queries, Sonnet 4.6 serves as Chief Judge. This brings the average cost of Phase 6 from $1.20 to approximately $0.30 per query.

Optimization 4 — Haiku for all structural tasks

Haiku 4.5 is approximately 20× cheaper per token than Sonnet 4.6 for input and 12× cheaper for output. The pipeline uses Haiku for every task that is mechanical rather than creative: the three research fan-out calls, three critique steps, three cross-examination rebuttals, the Devil's Advocate critique, and the citation re-prompt. Nine of the 23 LLM calls are Haiku.

Optimization 5 — Smart Tavily routing

Tavily web search is billed per request. Phase 1's Intel Agent explicitly decides how many searches to fire based on domain volatility. Classical tort law, established constitutional doctrine, and common-law contract rules are stable — searching for recent rulings on "what is negligence" returns noise, not signal. For these cases, searchCount: 0 saves the Tavily cost entirely.

Technical Infrastructure

OpenRouter as the unified LLM gateway

All five AI providers (Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, Tavily) are accessed through a single OpenRouter API key. OpenRouter exposes an OpenAI-compatible endpoint, so every model call in the pipeline uses identical request structure regardless of provider. This eliminates five separate SDK dependencies and means the pipeline can be updated to use a newer model version by changing a single string in the model constants map. 

// All models via one endpoint, one key
const M = {
  SONNET:  'anthropic/claude-sonnet-4.6',
  HAIKU:   'anthropic/claude-haiku-4.5',
  OPUS:    'anthropic/claude-opus-4.6',
  O4_MINI: 'openai/o4-mini',
  GEMINI:  'google/gemini-2.5-pro-preview',
};

Server-Sent Events for live streaming

The API endpoint uses SSE (Server-Sent Events) to push pipeline progress to the browser in real time. Each phase emits a phase_start event when it begins and a phase_complete event with structured data when it finishes. The Chief Judge's ruling is streamed token-by-token via stream_token events, so the user sees the ruling appear as it is generated — the pipeline doesn't go silent for 30 seconds and then dump everything at once.

Retry logic and network resilience

OpenRouter connections occasionally terminate mid-request (ECONNRESET, ETIMEDOUT). Every non-streaming LLM call is wrapped in a retry handler that attempts up to 2 retries with exponential backoff (1.5s, 3s). Streaming calls are not retried because partial output cannot be replayed cleanly — instead, Phase 6 falls back to a non-streaming Sonnet call with response_format: json_object if the stream fails.

The response_format + extended thinking conflict

Anthropic models do not allow response_format: { type: "json_object" } to be used simultaneously with extended thinking. When both are requested, the model ignores the JSON constraint and generates a markdown document. The llmOnce() function handles this by only setting response_format when thinking === 0: 

// response_format and thinking are mutually exclusive on Anthropic
...(json && thinking === 0 && { response_format: { type: 'json_object' } }),
...(thinking > 0 && model.startsWith('anthropic/') && {
  thinking: { type: 'enabled', budget_tokens: thinking },
}),

When thinking is enabled, the robust parseJSON() function handles the resulting free-text output with its 7-strategy fallback.

The search function scores every document against the query using keyword matching, term frequency in content and title, and a 3-point bonus when the document's legal area matches the case type from IntelBundle. Output components explained

  • Consensus badge — color-coded from green (STRONG_CONSENSUS) to red (FUNDAMENTAL_DISAGREEMENT). This is the first thing a user sees and immediately communicates how much the AI advocates agreed on the underlying facts and law.
  • Findings of fact — each finding labeled settled (green border) or disputed (red border), sourced to specific research bundle entries
  • Conclusions of law — each conclusion attributed to the advocate that originally raised it (plaintiff / defense / neutral / all)
  • Ruling text — streamed live; the final determination of who prevails and on what grounds
  • Epistemic markers — inline [settled], [disputed], [single_source] tags on key claims
  • Cited authorities — each source with its proposition; all verified against the ResearchBundle
  • Minority report (amber border, when present) — if sub-judges diverged significantly, the dissenting analysis
  • Devil's Advocate critique (red border) — what the system itself thinks might be wrong with the ruling
  • Citation integrity status — green if all verified, amber if citations were corrected by the re-prompt
  • Export — Markdown download (structured .md with all sections) or PDF (via print stylesheet)

File upload

Users can upload .txt or .md files instead of typing a query. The FileReader API reads the file client-side and populates the query textarea. This is useful for uploading contract documents, legal notices, case descriptions, or any document the user wants analyzed.

Summary: what makes this different

Most AI systems for legal questions are wrappers around a single model call. The Council of LLMs is fundamentally different in four ways:

  1. Adversarial architecture — truth is tested under cross-examination, not asserted by a single confident voice
  2. Model diversity — three AI providers, each with different training and failure modes, reduce the risk that systematic model bias goes unchallenged
  3. Programmatic verification — citations are not trusted because a model said they exist; they are verified against a known source set
  4. Epistemic transparency — the output tells you what the AI agreed on, what it disputed, and what it thinks it might have gotten wrong

The result is a system where the final ruling has been argued for, argued against, critiqued by the original advocate, cross-examined by opponents, evaluated by three specialist judges, synthesized by a Chief Judge who read all of it, programmatically fact-checked, and then challenged by a contrarian critic — all before the user sees it.

What the output is not This is not legal advice. The output of the Council of LLMs is structured reasoning about a legal situation based on available information. It should be used to understand the legal landscape of a situation, identify the strongest arguments on each side, and prepare informed questions for actual legal counsel — not to substitute for it.
